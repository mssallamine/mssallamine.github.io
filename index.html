About

Biography

I am a PhD researcher in development economics at Université Clermont Auvergne, affiliated with the Laboratoire d'Économie d'Orléans (LEO). My doctoral research, titled Essays on Macroeconomic Sovereignty in the Global South, examines how international institutions, global financial cycles, and resource dependence shape fiscal and monetary policy space in developing countries.

My research combines applied econometrics, panel data methods, event studies, local projections, spatial difference-in-differences, and geospatial analysis. I work primarily in R, Stata, Python, QGIS, Google Earth Engine, LaTeX, and Git/GitHub.

Before starting the PhD, I completed a Master 2 in Development Economics at Université Clermont Auvergne, with a thesis on the impact of hydropower projects in Virunga National Park on deforestation. I also completed an internship at Aide à la Décision Économique (ADE), a Belgian evaluation and consultancy firm, where I contributed to strategic and policy evaluations commissioned by the European Union.

PhD Thesis

Essays on Macroeconomic Sovereignty in the Global South
Université Clermont Auvergne / Laboratoire d'Économie d'Orléans (LEO)
2025–2028 (expected)

Supervised by Prof. Jean-Louis Combes and Prof. Pascale Motel Combes (Université Clermont Auvergne / LEO), with academic collaboration in Chad through Prof. Jean-Claude Kouladoum (Université de N'Djaména / LAREMA).

The thesis investigates the conditions under which developing countries can sustain productive public investment under IMF programme conditionality; how global financial shocks constrain domestic policy space; how resource dependence shapes fiscal resilience; and — through a geospatial working paper — the environmental effects of hydropower expansion near Virunga National Park, DRC.

Research

PhD thesis: Essays on Macroeconomic Sovereignty in the Global South — combining panel econometrics, local projections, event studies, and geospatial methods to study development policy in low- and middle-income countries.

Chapter 1 Preliminary Analysis · Working Paper in Preparation

Debt Sustainability or Developmental Fiscal Space? IMF Programmes and Public Investment in the Global South

This chapter examines whether IMF programme entry is associated with a short-run trade-off between fiscal stabilisation and public investment in low- and middle-income countries. Using programme timing from IMF MONA, fiscal variables from WEO/Fiscal Monitor, and public investment data from the IMF Investment and Capital Stock Dataset, the chapter studies whether fiscal balances improve while public investment is compressed. Additional checks examine private investment, total investment, growth, and HIPC/MDRI debt-relief timing.

Panel Data · Event Study · IMF MONA · Fiscal Policy · Public Investment
Chapter 2 Data Construction · Preliminary Design

Global Financial Cycles and Macroeconomic Sovereignty in Developing Countries

This chapter studies how U.S. rate shocks, changes in global risk, and shifts in dollar conditions create external financing pressures on developing countries. The empirical design examines how external debt exposure, reserve buffers, and debt-service pressure condition the erosion of macroeconomic sovereignty — including contractions in public investment, fiscal space, and growth — in low- and middle-income countries. Under construction using a local projections framework.

Local Projections · U.S. Rate Shocks · External Debt · Reserve Buffers · Macroeconomic Sovereignty
Chapter 3 Under Development

Resource Dependence, Fiscal Resilience, and Structural Transformation

This chapter will examine how commodity-price shocks and resource dependence shape fiscal resilience and structural transformation in developing economies. It will study whether resource-dependent countries are more vulnerable to global shocks, and whether institutions, fiscal rules, and diversification strategies mitigate those effects.

Resource Dependence · Commodity Shocks · Fiscal Resilience · Structural Transformation
Chapter 4 Working Paper · Preparing for Journal Submission

Hydropower and Forest Loss in a Conflict-Affected Protected Area: Evidence from the Matebe Project in Virunga

This working paper studies the environmental effects of the Matebe hydropower project near Virunga National Park in the Democratic Republic of Congo. Using a harmonised 5 km grid-cell panel for 2010–2020, it combines satellite-based forest-loss data with population, conflict, nighttime lights, terrain, road access, building/settlement indicators, and park-boundary diagnostics. The paper distinguishes construction and operational phases and shows that evidence of localised forest-loss patterns is sensitive to the choice of comparison group.

Spatial DiD · Remote Sensing · Deforestation · Hydropower · DRC · Virunga
